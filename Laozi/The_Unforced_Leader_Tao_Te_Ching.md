# The Eternal Way of the Unforced Leader

## (道德經)

### Chapter 1

道可道，非恆道。
Ways that can be spoken are not the eternal way.

名可名，非恆名。
Distinctions we can define are not eternal distinctions.

有名，萬物之母。
With distinction, it spans the world of forms, encompassing all things seen.

無名，萬物之始；
Without distinction, it precedes the world of forms, residing within the eternal unseen.

恆有欲，以觀其徼；
With desire, you observe the boundaries, its limits becoming clear.

故恆無欲，以觀其妙。
Without desire, you observe the inner workings, its nature becoming clear.

異名同謂。
Both of these are different distinctions, yet point to one way.

兩者同出，
For both of these have the same origin.

玄之又玄。
Within the depths of the unseen

眾妙之門。
lies the gateway to all profound mysteries.

### Chapter 2

天下皆知美之為美，
When beauty is distinguished,

斯惡已。
its shadow appears.

皆知善
When goodness is defined,

斯不善矣。
inadequacy is born.

有無之相生也，
Being and non-being create each other.

難易之相成也，
Difficult and easy shape each other.

長短之相刑也，
Long and short measure each other.

高下之相盈也，
High and low balance each other.

音聲之相和也，
Tone and sound weave each other into harmony.

先後之相隨也。
Before and after walk in sequence.

是以聖人居無為之事，
Therefore, the unforced leader dwells in unforced action, before the creation of opposites,

行不言之教；
and guides without imposing words.

萬物作焉而不辭，
All things arise, and the leader does not turn away.

生而不有，
They bring forth, but do not possess.

為而不恃，
They act, but do not require claim.

功成而弗居。
They accomplish, but do not dwell on accomplishment.

夫唯弗居，是以不去。
Because they do not dwell on it, it never leaves them.

### Chapter 3

不尚賢，使民不爭； 不貴難得之貨，使民不為盜；
Do not exalt the worthy,
and the people will not contend;
do not prize rare treasures,
and the people will not steal.

不見可欲，使民心不亂。
Do not display what can be desired,
and the people's hearts will not be disturbed.

是以聖人之治，虛其心，實其腹，
Therefore, the unforced leader
empties their minds and fills their bellies,

弱其志，強其骨。 常使民無知無欲。
softens their striving and strengthens their bones.

使夫智者不敢為也。
They keep the people free
from cunning and craving,
so the clever do not dare interfere.

為無為，則無不治。
Practice unforced action,
and nothing is left disordered.

### Chapter 4

道沖而用之或不盈，淵兮似萬物之宗；
The Tao is (like) the emptiness of a vessel;

挫其銳，解其紛，和其光，同其塵，湛兮似或存。
and in our employment of it we must be on our guard against all fulness.

吾不知誰之子，象帝之先。
How deep and unfathomable it is, as if it were the Honoured Ancestor of all things! We should blunt our sharp points, and unravel the complications of things; we should attemper our brightness, and bring ourselves into agreement with the obscurity of others. How pure and still the Tao is, as if it would ever so continue! I do not know whose son it is. It might appear to have been before God.

### Chapter 5

天地不仁，以萬物為芻狗；
Heaven and earth do not act from (the impulse of) any wish to be benevolent;

聖人不仁，以百姓為芻狗。
they deal with all things as the dogs of grass are dealt with. The sages do not act from (any wish to be) benevolent; they deal with the people as the dogs of grass are dealt with.

天地之間，其猶橐籥乎？ 虛而不屈，動而愈出。
May not the space between heaven and earth be compared to a bellows?

多言數窮，不如守中。
'Tis emptied, yet it loses not its power; 'Tis moved again, and sends forth air the more. Much speech to swift exhaustion lead we see; Your inner being guard, and keep it free.

### Chapter 6

谷神不死，是謂玄牝。
The valley spirit dies not, aye the same;

玄牝之門，是謂天地根。
The female mystery thus do we name.

緜緜若存，用之不勤。
Its gate, from which at first they issued forth, Is called the root from which grew heaven and earth. Long and unbroken does its power remain, Used gently, and without the touch of pain.

### Chapter 7

天長地久。
Heaven is long-enduring and earth continues long.

天地所以能長且久者，以其不自生，
The reason why heaven and earth are able to endure and continue thus long is because they do not live of, or for, themselves. This is how they are able to continue and endure.

故能長生。 是以聖人後其身而身先；
Therefore the sage puts his own person last, and yet it is found in the foremost place;

外其身而身存。
he treats his person as if it were foreign to him, and yet that person is preserved.

非以其無私邪，故能成其私。
Is it not because he has no personal and private ends, that therefore such ends are realised?

### Chapter 8

上善若水。
The highest excellence is like (that of) water.

水善利萬物而不爭，處眾人之所惡，
The excellence of water appears in its benefiting all things, and in its occupying, without striving (to the contrary), the low place which all men dislike. Hence (its way) is near to (that of) the Tao.

故幾於道。
The excellence of a residence is in (the suitability of) the place; that of the mind is in abysmal stillness; that of associations is in their being with the virtuous; that of government is in its securing good order; that of (the conduct of) affairs is in its ability; and that of (the initiation of) any movement is in its timeliness.

居善地，心善淵，與善仁，言善信，正善治，事善能，動善時。 夫唯不爭，故無尤。
And when (one with the highest excellence) does not wrangle (about his low position), no one finds fault with him.

### Chapter 9

持而盈之，不如其已； 揣而梲之，不可長保。
It is better to leave a vessel unfilled, than to attempt to carry it when it is full.

金玉滿堂，莫之能守；
If you keep feeling a point that has been sharpened, the point cannot long preserve its sharpness.

富貴而驕，自遺其咎。
When gold and jade fill the hall, their possessor cannot keep them safe.

功遂身退，天之道。
When wealth and honours lead to arrogancy, this brings its evil on itself. When the work is done, and one's name is becoming distinguished, to withdraw into obscurity is the way of Heaven.

### Chapter 10

載營魄抱一，能無離乎？
When the intelligent and animal souls are held together in one embrace, they can be kept from separating.

專氣致柔，能嬰兒乎？
When one gives undivided attention to the (vital) breath, and brings it to the utmost degree of pliancy, he can become as a (tender) babe.

滌除玄覽，能無疵乎？
When he has cleansed away the most mysterious sights (of his imagination), he can become without a flaw.

愛國治民，能無知乎？
In loving the people and ruling the state, cannot he proceed without any (purpose of) action?

天門開闔，能為雌乎？
In the opening and shutting of his gates of heaven, cannot he do so as a female bird?

明白四達，能無為乎？
While his intelligence reaches in every direction, cannot he (appear to) be without knowledge?

生之，
(The Tao) produces (all things) and nourishes them;

畜之。
it produces them and does not claim them as its own;

生而不有，為而不恃，長而不宰，是謂玄德。
it does all, and yet does not boast of it; it presides over all, and yet does not control them. This is what is called 'The mysterious Quality' (of the Tao).

### Chapter 11

三十輻共一轂，當其無，有車之用。
The thirty spokes unite in the one nave;

埏埴以為器，當其無，有器之用。
but it is on the empty space (for the axle), that the use of the wheel depends.

鑿戶牖以為室，當其無，有室之用。
Clay is fashioned into vessels;

故有之以為利，無之以為用。
but it is on their empty hollowness, that their use depends. The door and windows are cut out (from the walls) to form an apartment; but it is on the empty space (within), that its use depends. Therefore, what has a (positive) existence serves for profitable adaptation, and what has not that for (actual) usefulness.

### Chapter 12

五色令人目盲，五音令人耳聾，五味令人口爽，馳騁畋獵令人心發狂，
Colour's five hues from th' eyes their sight will take;

難得之貨令人行妨。
Music's five notes the ears as deaf can make; The flavours five deprive the mouth of taste; The chariot course, and the wild hunting waste Make mad the mind; and objects rare and strange, Sought for, men's conduct will to evil change.

是以聖人為腹不為目，故去彼取此。
Therefore the sage seeks to satisfy (the craving of) the belly, and not the (insatiable longing of the) eyes. He puts from him the latter, and prefers to seek the former.

### Chapter 13

寵辱若驚，貴大患若身。 何謂寵辱若驚？
Favour and disgrace would seem equally to be feared;

寵為下，得之若驚，失之若驚，是謂寵辱若驚。 何謂貴大患若身？
honour and great calamity, to be regarded as personal conditions (of the same kind).

吾所以有大患者，為吾有身，
What is meant by speaking thus of favour and disgrace?

及吾無身，
Disgrace is being in a low position (after the enjoyment of favour). The getting that (favour) leads to the apprehension (of losing it), and the losing it leads to the fear of (still greater calamity):--this is what is meant by saying that favour and disgrace would seem equally to be feared.

吾有何患？
And what is meant by saying that honour and great calamity are to be (similarly) regarded as personal conditions?

故貴以身為天下，若可寄天下；
What makes me liable to great calamity is my having the body (which I call myself); if I had not the body, what great calamity could come to me?

愛以身為天下，若可託天下。
Therefore he who would administer the kingdom, honouring it as he honours his own person, may be employed to govern it, and he who would administer it with the love which he bears to his own person may be entrusted with it.

### Chapter 14

視之不見名曰夷，聽之不聞名曰希，搏之不得名曰微。 此三者，不可致詰，故混而為一。 其上不皦，其下不昧。
We look at it, and we do not see it, and we name it 'the Equable.' We listen to it, and we do not hear it, and we name it 'the Inaudible.' We try to grasp it, and do not get hold of it, and we name it 'the Subtle.' With these three qualities, it cannot be made the subject of description;

繩繩不可名，復歸於無物。 是謂無狀之狀，無物之象，
and hence we blend them together and obtain The One.

是謂惚恍。
Its upper part is not bright, and its lower part is not obscure.

迎之不見其首，隨之不見其後。
Ceaseless in its action, it yet cannot be named, and then it again returns and becomes nothing.

執古之道，以御今之有。
This is called the Form of the Formless, and the Semblance of the Invisible; this is called the Fleeting and Indeterminable.

能知古始，是謂道紀。
We meet it and do not see its Front; we follow it, and do not see its Back. When we can lay hold of the Tao of old to direct the things of the present day, and are able to know it as it was of old in the beginning, this is called (unwinding) the clue of Tao.

### Chapter 15

古之善為士者，微妙玄通，深不可識。 夫唯不可識，故強為之容。
The skilful masters (of the Tao) in old times, with a subtle and exquisite penetration, comprehended its mysteries, and were deep (also) so as to elude men's knowledge.

豫焉若冬涉川， 猶兮若畏四鄰，
As they were thus beyond men's knowledge, I will make an effort to describe of what sort they appeared to be.

儼兮其若容，渙兮若冰之將釋，敦兮其若樸，曠兮其若谷，混兮其若濁。
Shrinking looked they like those who wade through a stream in winter;

孰能濁以靜之徐清？
irresolute like those who are afraid of all around them;

孰能安以久動之徐生？
grave like a guest (in awe of his host); evanescent like ice that is melting away; unpretentious like wood that has not been fashioned into anything; vacant like a valley, and dull like muddy water.

保此道者不欲盈，
Who can (make) the muddy water (clear)? Let it be still, and it will gradually become clear. Who can secure the condition of rest? Let movement go on, and the condition of rest will gradually arise.

夫唯不盈，故能蔽不新成。
They who preserve this method of the Tao do not wish to be full (of themselves). It is through their not being full of themselves that they can afford to seem worn and not appear new and complete.

### Chapter 16

致虛極，守靜篤。 萬物並作，吾以觀復。
The (state of) vacancy should be brought to the utmost degree, and that of stillness guarded with unwearying vigour.

夫物芸芸，各復歸其根。 歸根曰靜，是謂復命。
All things alike go through their processes of activity, and (then) we see them return (to their original state).

復命曰常， 知常曰明。
When things (in the vegetable world) have displayed their luxuriant growth, we see each of them return to its root.

不知常，妄作凶。 知常容，
This returning to their root is what we call the state of stillness;

容乃公，
and that stillness may be called a reporting that they have fulfilled their appointed end.

公乃全，
The report of that fulfilment is the regular, unchanging rule.

全乃天，
To know that unchanging rule is to be intelligent;

天乃道，
not to know it leads to wild movements and evil issues.

道乃久，
The knowledge of that unchanging rule produces a (grand) capacity and forbearance, and that capacity and forbearance lead to a community (of feeling with all things).

沒身不殆。
From this community of feeling comes a kingliness of character; and he who is king-like goes on to be heaven-like. In that likeness to heaven he possesses the Tao. Possessed of the Tao, he endures long; and to the end of his bodily life, is exempt from all danger of decay.

### Chapter 17

太上，下知有之，
In the highest antiquity, (the people) did not know that there were (their rulers).

其次，親而譽之，
In the next age they loved them and praised them.

其次，畏之，
In the next they feared them; in the next they despised them. Thus it was that when faith (in the Tao) was deficient (in the rulers) a want of faith in them ensued (in the people).

其次，侮之。 信不足焉，有不信焉。
How irresolute did those (earliest rulers) appear, showing (by their reticence) the importance which they set upon their words!

悠兮其貴言，功成事遂，百姓皆謂：我自然。
Their work was done and their undertakings were successful, while the people all said, 'We are as we are, of ourselves!'

### Chapter 18

大道廢，有仁義；
When the Great Tao (Way or Method) ceased to be observed, benevolence and righteousness came into vogue.

智慧出，有大偽；
(Then) appeared wisdom and shrewdness, and there ensued great hypocrisy.

六親不和，有孝慈；
When harmony no longer prevailed throughout the six kinships, filial sons found their manifestation;

國家昏亂，有忠臣。
when the states and clans fell into disorder, loyal ministers appeared.

### Chapter 19

絕聖棄智，民利百倍；
If we could renounce our sageness and discard our wisdom, it would be better for the people a hundredfold.

絕仁棄義，民復孝慈；
If we could renounce our benevolence and discard our righteousness, the people would again become filial and kindly.

絕巧棄利，盜賊無有。
If we could renounce our artful contrivances and discard our (scheming for) gain, there would be no thieves nor robbers.

此三者以為文不足，故令有所屬：見素抱樸，少私寡欲。
Those three methods (of government) Thought olden ways in elegance did fail And made these names their want of worth to veil; But simple views, and courses plain and true Would selfish ends and many lusts eschew.

### Chapter 20

絕學無憂，唯之與阿，相去幾何？ 善之與惡，相去若何？
When we renounce learning we have no troubles.

人之所畏，不可不畏。 荒兮其未央哉！
The (ready) 'yes,' and (flattering) 'yea;'-- Small is the difference they display.

眾人熙熙，如享太牢，如春登臺。 我獨泊兮其未兆，如嬰兒之未孩；
But mark their issues, good and ill;-- What space the gulf between shall fill?

儽儽兮若無所歸。 眾人皆有餘，而我獨若遺。
What all men fear is indeed to be feared;

我愚人之心也哉！ 沌沌兮，
but how wide and without end is the range of questions (asking to be discussed)!

俗人昭昭，
The multitude of men look satisfied and pleased;

我獨昏昏。
as if enjoying a full banquet, as if mounted on a tower in spring.

俗人察察，
I alone seem listless and still, my desires having as yet given no indication of their presence.

我獨悶悶。
I am like an infant which has not yet smiled.

澹兮其若海，
I look dejected and forlorn, as if I had no home to go to.

飂兮若無止。
The multitude of men all have enough and to spare. I alone seem to have lost everything. My mind is that of a stupid man; I am in a state of chaos.

眾人皆有以，
Ordinary men look bright and intelligent, while I alone seem to be benighted.

而我獨頑似鄙。
They look full of discrimination, while I alone am dull and confused.

我獨異於人，而貴食母。
I seem to be carried about as on the sea, drifting as if I had nowhere to rest. All men have their spheres of action, while I alone seem dull and incapable, like a rude borderer. (Thus) I alone am different from other men, but I value the nursing-mother (the Tao).

### Chapter 21

孔德之容，惟道是從。
The grandest forms of active force From Tao come, their only source.

道之為物，惟恍惟惚。
Who can of Tao the nature tell?

惚兮恍兮，其中有象；
Our sight it flies, our touch as well.

恍兮惚兮，其中有物。
Eluding sight, eluding touch, The forms of things all in it crouch;

窈兮冥兮，其中有精；
Eluding touch, eluding sight, There are their semblances, all right. Profound it is, dark and obscure; Things' essences all there endure. Those essences the truth enfold Of what, when seen, shall then be told. Now it is so; 'twas so of old. Its name--what passes not away; So, in their beautiful array, Things form and never know decay.

其精甚真，其中有信。
Its essence is most real; within it is trustworthiness.

自今及古，其名不去，
From now to ancient times, its name has never departed.

以閱眾甫。
By it, the beginning of all things is perceived.

吾何以知眾甫之狀哉？ 以此。
By this (nature of the Tao).

### Chapter 22

曲則全，
The partial becomes complete;

枉則直，
the crooked, straight;

窪則盈，
the empty, full; the worn out, new. He whose (desires) are few gets them; he whose (desires) are many goes astray.

敝則新，
Therefore the sage holds in his embrace the one thing (of humility), and manifests it to all the world.

少則得，多則惑。
He is free from self-display, and therefore he shines; from self-assertion, and therefore he is distinguished; from self-boasting, and therefore his merit is acknowledged; from self-complacency, and therefore he acquires superiority. It is because he is thus free from striving that therefore no one in the world is able to strive with him.

是以聖人抱一為天下式。
Therefore the sage holds to the One as a model for all under heaven.

不自見故明，
Not displaying himself, he is clear.

不自是故彰，
Not asserting himself, he is distinguished.

不自伐故有功，
Not boasting, he has real accomplishment.

不自矜故長。
Not exalting himself, he endures.

夫唯不爭，故天下莫能與之爭。
Because he does not contend, none under heaven can contend with him.

古之所謂曲則全者，豈虛言哉！
The ancient saying that "the bent becomes whole" is not empty talk.

誠全而歸之。
In true wholeness, all returns to it.

### Chapter 23

希言自然。
Abstaining from speech marks him who is obeying the spontaneity of his nature.

故飄風不終朝，驟雨不終日。
A violent wind does not last for a whole morning;

孰為此者？
a sudden rain does not last for the whole day.

天地。
To whom is it that these (two) things are owing?

天地尚不能久，而況於人乎？
To Heaven and Earth.

故從事於道者，道者同於道，
If Heaven and Earth cannot make such (spasmodic) actings last long, how much less can man!

德者同於德，
Therefore when one is making the Tao his business, those who are also pursuing it, agree with him in it, and those who are making the manifestation of its course their object agree with him in that;

失者同於失。
while even those who are failing in both these things agree with him where they fail.

同於道者，道亦樂得之；
Hence, those with whom he agrees as to the Tao have the happiness of attaining to it;

同於德者，德亦樂得之；
those with whom he agrees as to its manifestation have the happiness of attaining to it;

同於失者，失亦樂得之。
and those with whom he agrees in their failure have also the happiness of attaining (to the Tao).

信不足焉，有不信焉。
(But) when there is not faith sufficient (on his part), a want of faith (in him) ensues (on the part of the others).

### Chapter 24

企者不立，
He who stands on his tiptoes does not stand firm;

跨者不行，自見者不明，自是者不彰，自伐者無功，自矜者不長。
he who stretches his legs does not walk (easily).

其在道也，曰餘食贅行。
(So), he who displays himself does not shine;

物或惡之，故有道者不處。
he who asserts his own views is not distinguished; he who vaunts himself does not find his merit acknowledged; he who is self-conceited has no superiority allowed to him. Such conditions, viewed from the standpoint of the Tao, are like remnants of food, or a tumour on the body, which all dislike. Hence those who pursue (the course) of the Tao do not adopt and allow them.

### Chapter 25

有物混成，先天地生。
There was something undefined and complete, coming into existence before Heaven and Earth.

寂兮寥兮，獨立而不改，
How still it was and formless, standing alone, and undergoing no change, reaching everywhere and in no danger (of being exhausted)!

周行而不殆，可以為天下母。
It may be regarded as the Mother of all things.

吾不知其名， 字之曰道，
I do not know its name, and I give it the designation of the Tao (the Way or Course).

強為之名曰大。
Making an effort (further) to give it a name I call it The Great.

大曰逝，
Great, it passes on (in constant flow).

逝曰遠，遠曰反。
Passing on, it becomes remote.

故道大，天大，地大，王亦大。
Having become remote, it returns. Therefore the Tao is great; Heaven is great; Earth is great; and the (sage) king is also great. In the universe there are four that are great, and the (sage) king is one of them.

域中有四大，
Man takes his law from the Earth;

而王居其一焉。
the Earth takes its law from Heaven;

人法地，地法天，天法道，道法自然。
Heaven takes its law from the Tao. The law of the Tao is its being what it is.

### Chapter 26

重為輕根，靜為躁君。
Gravity is the root of lightness;

是以聖人終日行不離輜重。
stillness, the ruler of movement.

雖有榮觀，燕處超然。
Therefore a wise prince, marching the whole day, does not go far from his baggage waggons.

奈何萬乘之主，而以身輕天下？
Although he may have brilliant prospects to look at, he quietly remains (in his proper place), indifferent to them.

輕則失本，躁則失君。
How should the lord of a myriad chariots carry himself lightly before the kingdom? If he do act lightly, he has lost his root (of gravity); if he proceed to active movement, he will lose his throne.

### Chapter 27

善行無轍迹，
The skilful traveller leaves no traces of his wheels or footsteps;

善言無瑕讁；
the skilful speaker says nothing that can be found fault with or blamed;

善數不用籌策；
the skilful reckoner uses no tallies;

善閉無關楗而不可開，善結無繩約而不可解。
the skilful closer needs no bolts or bars, while to open what he has shut will be impossible;

是以聖人常善救人，故無棄人；
the skilful binder uses no strings or knots, while to unloose what he has bound will be impossible. In the same way the sage is always skilful at saving men, and so he does not cast away any man; he is always skilful at saving things, and so he does not cast away anything. This is called 'Hiding the light of his procedure.'

常善救物，故無棄物，是謂襲明。 故善人者，不善人之師；
Therefore the man of skill is a master (to be looked up to) by him who has not the skill;

不善人者，善人之資。
and he who has not the skill is the helper of (the reputation of) him who has the skill.

不貴其師，不愛其資，雖智大迷，
If the one did not honour his master, and the other did not rejoice in his helper, an (observer), though intelligent, might greatly err about them.

是謂要妙。
This is called 'The utmost degree of mystery.'

### Chapter 28

知其雄，守其雌，為天下谿。
Who knows his manhood's strength, Yet still his female feebleness maintains;

為天下谿，常德不離，復歸於嬰兒。
As to one channel flow the many drains, All come to him, yea, all beneath the sky.

知其白，守其黑，為天下式。
Thus he the constant excellence retains; The simple child again, free from all stains.

為天下式，常德不忒，
Who knows how white attracts, Yet always keeps himself within black's shade, The pattern of humility displayed, Displayed in view of all beneath the sky;

復歸於無極。
He in the unchanging excellence arrayed, Endless return to man's first state has made.

知其榮，守其辱，為天下谷，常德乃足，復歸於樸。
Who knows how glory shines, Yet loves disgrace, nor e'er for it is pale;

樸散則為器，聖人用之，則為官長，
Behold his presence in a spacious vale, To which men come from all beneath the sky. The unchanging excellence completes its tale; The simple infant man in him we hail.

故大制不割。
The unwrought material, when divided and distributed, forms vessels. The sage, when employed, becomes the Head of all the Officers (of government); and in his greatest regulations he employs no violent measures.

### Chapter 29

將欲取天下而為之，吾見其不得已。
If any one should wish to get the kingdom for himself, and to effect this by what he does, I see that he will not succeed.

天下神器，
The kingdom is a spirit-like thing, and cannot be got by active doing. He who would so win it destroys it; he who would hold it in his grasp loses it.

不可為也，為者敗之，執者失之。
The course and nature of things is such that What was in front is now behind; What warmed anon we freezing find. Strength is of weakness oft the spoil; The store in ruins mocks our toil.

故物或行或隨，或歔或吹。 或強或羸，或挫或隳。 是以聖人去甚，去奢，去泰。
Hence the sage puts away excessive effort, extravagance, and easy indulgence.

### Chapter 30

以道佐人主者，不以兵強天下。
He who would assist a lord of men in harmony with the Tao will not assert his mastery in the kingdom by force of arms.

其事好還。
Such a course is sure to meet with its proper return.

師之所處，荊棘生焉。 大軍之後，必有凶年。
Wherever a host is stationed, briars and thorns spring up.

善有果而已，不敢以取強。
In the sequence of great armies there are sure to be bad years.

果而勿矜，果而勿伐，果而勿驕。
A skilful (commander) strikes a decisive blow, and stops.

果而不得已，果而勿強。
He does not dare (by continuing his operations) to assert and complete his mastery. He will strike the blow, but will be on his guard against being vain or boastful or arrogant in consequence of it. He strikes it as a matter of necessity; he strikes it, but not from a wish for mastery.

物壯則老，是謂不道，不道早已。
When things have attained their strong maturity they become old. This may be said to be not in accordance with the Tao: and what is not in accordance with it soon comes to an end.

### Chapter 31

夫佳兵者，不祥之器，物或惡之，故有道者不處。
Now arms, however beautiful, are instruments of evil omen, hateful, it may be said, to all creatures.

君子居則貴左，用兵則貴右。
Therefore they who have the Tao do not like to employ them.

兵者不祥之器，非君子之器，不得已而用之，恬淡為上。
The superior man ordinarily considers the left hand the most honourable place, but in time of war the right hand.

勝而不美，而美之者，是樂殺人。
Those sharp weapons are instruments of evil omen, and not the instruments of the superior man;--he uses them only on the compulsion of necessity.

夫樂殺人者，則不可以得志於天下矣。
Calm and repose are what he prizes;

吉事尚左，凶事尚右。
victory (by force of arms) is to him undesirable.

偏將軍居左，上將軍居右，言以喪禮處之。
To consider this desirable would be to delight in the slaughter of men;

殺人之眾，以哀悲泣之，戰勝，以喪禮處之。
and he who delights in the slaughter of men cannot get his will in the kingdom. On occasions of festivity to be on the left hand is the prized position; on occasions of mourning, the right hand. The second in command of the army has his place on the left; the general commanding in chief has his on the right;--his place, that is, is assigned to him as in the rites of mourning. He who has killed multitudes of men should weep for them with the bitterest grief; and the victor in battle has his place (rightly) according to those rites.

### Chapter 32

道常無名，樸雖小，天下莫能臣也。 侯王若能守之，萬物將自賓。
The Tao, considered as unchanging, has no name.

天地相合，以降甘露，民莫之令而自均。
Though in its primordial simplicity it may be small, the whole world dares not deal with (one embodying) it as a minister. If a feudal prince or the king could guard and hold it, all would spontaneously submit themselves to him.

始制有名，名亦既有，夫亦將知止，知止所以不殆。
Heaven and Earth (under its guidance) unite together and send down the sweet dew, which, without the directions of men, reaches equally everywhere as of its own accord.

譬道之在天下，猶川谷之於江海。
As soon as it proceeds to action, it has a name. When it once has that name, (men) can know to rest in it. When they know to rest in it, they can be free from all risk of failure and error. The relation of the Tao to all the world is like that of the great rivers and seas to the streams from the valleys.

### Chapter 33

知人者智，自知者明。
He who knows other men is discerning;

勝人者有力，自勝者強。
he who knows himself is intelligent.

知足者富。
He who overcomes others is strong; he who overcomes himself is mighty. He who is satisfied with his lot is rich; he who goes on acting with energy has a (firm) will.

強行者有志。 不失其所者久。
He who does not fail in the requirements of his position, continues long;

死而不亡者壽。
he who dies and yet does not perish, has longevity.

### Chapter 34

大道氾兮，其可左右。 萬物恃之而生而不辭，功成不名有。
All-pervading is the Great Tao!

衣養萬物而不為主，常無欲，可名於小；
It may be found on the left hand and on the right.

萬物歸焉而不為主，可名為大。
All things depend on it for their production, which it gives to them, not one refusing obedience to it. When its work is accomplished, it does not claim the name of having done it. It clothes all things as with a garment, and makes no assumption of being their lord;--it may be named in the smallest things. All things return (to their root and disappear), and do not know that it is it which presides over their doing so;--it may be named in the greatest things.

以其終不自為大，故能成其大。
Hence the sage is able (in the same way) to accomplish his great achievements. It is through his not making himself great that he can accomplish them.

### Chapter 35

執大象，天下往。
To him who holds in his hands the Great Image (of the invisible Tao), the whole world repairs.

往而不害，安平太。
Men resort to him, and receive no hurt, but (find) rest, peace, and the feeling of ease.

樂與餌，過客止。
Music and dainties will make the passing guest stop (for a time).

道之出口，淡乎其無味，視之不足見，聽之不足聞，用之不足既。
But though the Tao as it comes from the mouth, seems insipid and has no flavour, though it seems not worth being looked at or listened to, the use of it is inexhaustible.

### Chapter 36

將欲歙之，必固張之； 將欲弱之，必固強之；
When one is about to take an inspiration, he is sure to make a (previous) expiration;

將欲廢之，必固興之；
when he is going to weaken another, he will first strengthen him;

將欲奪之，必固與之。
when he is going to overthrow another, he will first have raised him up;

是謂微明。
when he is going to despoil another, he will first have made gifts to him:--this is called 'Hiding the light (of his procedure).'

柔弱勝剛強。
The soft overcomes the hard;

魚不可脫於淵，國之利器不可以示人。
and the weak the strong. Fishes should not be taken from the deep; instruments for the profit of a state should not be shown to the people.

### Chapter 37

道常無為
The Tao in its regular course does nothing (for the sake of doing it), and so there is nothing which it does not do.

而無不為。
If princes and kings were able to maintain it, all things would of themselves be transformed by them.

侯王若能守之，萬物將自化。 化而欲作，吾將鎮之以無名之樸。
If this transformation became to me an object of desire, I would express the desire by the nameless simplicity.

無名之樸，夫亦將無欲。
Simplicity without a name Is free from all external aim. With no desire, at rest and still, All things go right as of their will.

不欲以靜，天下將自定。
Without desire and in stillness, all under heaven settles itself.

### Chapter 38

上德不德，是以有德；
The highest virtue does not perform virtue, and so it is truly virtuous.

下德不失德，是以無德。
Lower virtue cannot stop clinging to virtue, and so it lacks true virtue.

上德無為而無以為；
Higher virtue acts without forcing and without agenda.

上仁為之而無以為；
Higher benevolence acts, yet still without agenda.

上義為之而有以為；
Higher righteousness acts with a purpose.

上禮為之而莫之應，則攘臂而扔之。
Higher ritual acts, and when no one responds, it rolls up its sleeves and imposes itself.

故失道而後德，失德而後仁，失仁而後義，失義而後禮。
So when the Way is lost, virtue appears; when virtue is lost, benevolence appears; then righteousness; then ritual.

夫禮者，忠信之薄，而亂之首。
Ritual is the thinning of trust and sincerity, and the beginning of disorder.

前識者，道之華，而愚之始。
Foreknowledge is the flower of the Way, and the beginning of folly.

是以大丈夫處其厚，不居其薄；
So the grounded person dwells in depth, not surface;

處其實，不居其華。
in substance, not appearance;

故去彼取此。
and leaves the latter for the former.

### Chapter 39

昔之得一者，
The things which from of old have got the One (the Tao) are--

天得一以清，地得一以寧，神得一以靈，谷得一以盈，萬物得一以生，侯王得一以為天下貞。
Heaven which by it is bright and pure;

其致之，
Earth rendered thereby firm and sure; Spirits with powers by it supplied; Valleys kept full throughout their void All creatures which through it do live Princes and kings who from it get The model which to all they give.

天無以清將恐裂，
All these are the results of the One (Tao).

地無以寧將恐發，神無以靈將恐歇，谷無以盈將恐竭，萬物無以生將恐滅，侯王無以貴高將恐蹶。
If heaven were not thus pure, it soon would rend;

故貴以賤為本，高以下為基。
If earth were not thus sure, 'twould break and bend;

是以侯王自稱孤﹑寡﹑不穀。
Without these powers, the spirits soon would fail;

此非以賤為本邪？
If not so filled, the drought would parch each vale;

非乎？
Without that life, creatures would pass away;

故致數輿無輿，不欲琭琭如玉，珞珞如石。
Princes and kings, without that moral sway, However grand and high, would all decay. Thus it is that dignity finds its (firm) root in its (previous) meanness, and what is lofty finds its stability in the lowness (from which it rises). Hence princes and kings call themselves 'Orphans,' 'Men of small virtue,' and as 'Carriages without a nave.' Is not this an acknowledgment that in their considering themselves mean they see the foundation of their dignity? So it is that in the enumeration of the different parts of a carriage we do not come on what makes it answer the ends of a carriage. They do not wish to show themselves elegant-looking as jade, but (prefer) to be coarse-looking as an (ordinary) stone.

### Chapter 40

反者道之動，
The movement of the Tao By contraries proceeds;

弱者道之用。
And weakness marks the course Of Tao's mighty deeds.

天下萬物生於有，有生於無。
All things under heaven sprang from It as existing (and named); that existence sprang from It as non-existent (and not named).

### Chapter 41

上士聞道，勤而行之； 中士聞道，若存若亡；
Scholars of the highest class, when they hear about the Tao, earnestly carry it into practice.

下士聞道，大笑之。 不笑不足以為道。
Scholars of the middle class, when they have heard about it, seem now to keep it and now to lose it.

故建言有之： 明道若昧，
Scholars of the lowest class, when they have heard about it, laugh greatly at it.

進道若退，
If it were not (thus) laughed at, it would not be fit to be the Tao.

夷道若纇， 上德若谷， 大白若辱， 廣德若不足，
Therefore the sentence-makers have thus expressed themselves:--

建德若偷，
'The Tao, when brightest seen, seems light to lack;

質真若渝，
Who progress in it makes, seems drawing back;

大方無隅，
Its even way is like a rugged track.

大器晚成，
Its highest virtue from the vale doth rise; Its greatest beauty seems to offend the eyes; And he has most whose lot the least supplies. Its firmest virtue seems but poor and low; Its solid truth seems change to undergo; Its largest square doth yet no corner show A vessel great, it is the slowest made; Loud is its sound, but never word it said; A semblance great, the shadow of a shade.'

大音希聲， 大象無形，
The Tao is hidden, and has no name;

道隱無名。 夫唯道，善貸且成。
but it is the Tao which is skilful at imparting (to all things what they need) and making them complete.

### Chapter 42

道生一，一生二，二生三，三生萬物。
The Tao produced One;

萬物負陰而抱陽，沖氣以為和。
One produced Two;

人之所惡，唯孤﹑寡﹑不穀，而王公以為稱。
Two produced Three;

故物或損之而益，或益之而損。
Three produced All things. All things leave behind them the Obscurity (out of which they have come), and go forward to embrace the Brightness (into which they have emerged), while they are harmonised by the Breath of Vacancy.

人之所教，我亦教之。
What men dislike is to be orphans, to have little virtue, to be as carriages without naves; and yet these are the designations which kings and princes use for themselves. So it is that some things are increased by being diminished, and others are diminished by being increased.

強梁者不得其死，吾將以為教父。
What other men (thus) teach, I also teach. The violent and strong do not die their natural death. I will make this the basis of my teaching.

### Chapter 43

天下之至柔，馳騁天下之至堅。
The softest thing in the world dashes against and overcomes the hardest;

無有入無間，吾是以知無為之有益。
that which has no (substantial) existence enters where there is no crevice. I know hereby what advantage belongs to doing nothing (with a purpose).

不言之教，無為之益，天下希及之。
There are few in the world who attain to the teaching without words, and the advantage arising from non-action.

### Chapter 44

名與身孰親？
Or fame or life, Which do you hold more dear?

身與貨孰多？
Or life or wealth, To which would you adhere? Keep life and lose those other things; Keep them and lose your life:--which brings Sorrow and pain more near?

得與亡孰病？
Thus we may see, Who cleaves to fame Rejects what is more great;

是故甚愛必大費，多藏必厚亡，
Who loves large stores Gives up the richer state.

知足不辱，知止不殆，可以長久。
Who is content Needs fear no shame. Who knows to stop Incurs no blame. From danger free Long live shall he.

### Chapter 45

大成若缺，其用不弊。
Who thinks his great achievements poor Shall find his vigour long endure.

大盈若沖，其用不窮。
Of greatest fulness, deemed a void, Exhaustion ne'er shall stem the tide.

大直若屈，其用不居。
Do thou what's straight still crooked deem; Thy greatest art still stupid seem, And eloquence a stammering scream.

大巧若拙，其用不輟。 大辯若訥，其用不差。
Constant action overcomes cold;

躁勝寒，靜勝熱。
being still overcomes heat.

清靜為天下正。
Purity and stillness give the correct law to all under heaven.

### Chapter 46

天下有道，卻走馬以糞。
When the Tao prevails in the world, they send back their swift horses to (draw) the dung-carts.

天下無道，戎馬生於郊。
When the Tao is disregarded in the world, the war-horses breed in the border lands.

禍莫大於不知足；
There is no guilt greater than to sanction ambition;

咎莫大於欲得。
no calamity greater than to be discontented with one's lot;

故知足之足，常足矣。
no fault greater than the wish to be getting. Therefore the sufficiency of contentment is an enduring and unchanging sufficiency.

### Chapter 47

不出戶，知天下；
Without stepping outside the door, one understands what unfolds under heaven.

不闚牖，見天道。
Without looking out the window, one sees the Way of Heaven.

其出彌遠，其知彌少。
The farther one goes outward, the less one understands.

是以聖人不行而知，不見而名，
Therefore, the unforced leader knows without wandering;

不為而成。
distinguishes without looking; and accomplishes without forcing.

### Chapter 48

為學日益，
He who devotes himself to learning (seeks) from day to day to increase (his knowledge);

為道日損。
he who devotes himself to the Tao (seeks) from day to day to diminish (his doing).

損之又損，以至於無為。 無為而無不為。
He diminishes it and again diminishes it, till he arrives at doing nothing (on purpose).

取天下常以無事，
Having arrived at this point of non-action, there is nothing which he does not do.

及其有事，
He who gets as his own all under heaven does so by giving himself no trouble (with that end).

不足以取天下。
If one take trouble (with that end), he is not equal to getting as his own all under heaven.

### Chapter 49

聖人無常心，以百姓心為心。 善者，吾善之；
The sage has no invariable mind of his own;

不善者，吾亦善之，
he makes the mind of the people his mind.

德善。
To those who are good (to me), I am good;

信者，吾信之；
and to those who are not good (to me), I am also good;--and thus (all) get to be good.

不信者，吾亦信之，德信。
To those who are sincere (with me), I am sincere;

聖人在天下歙歙，為天下渾其心，
and to those who are not sincere (with me), I am also sincere;--and thus (all) get to be sincere.

百姓皆注其耳目,
The sage has in the world an appearance of indecision, and keeps his mind in a state of indifference to all.

聖人皆孩之。
The people all keep their eyes and ears directed to him, and he deals with them all as his children.

### Chapter 50

出生入死。
Men come forth and live; they enter (again) and die.

生之徒，十有三； 死之徒，十有三； 人之生，動之死地，亦十有三。 夫何故？ 以其生生之厚。
Of every ten three are ministers of life (to themselves);

蓋聞善攝生者，陸行不遇兕虎，入軍不被甲兵； 兕無所投其角，虎無所措其爪，兵無所容其刃。 夫何故？ 以其無死地。
and three are ministers of death. There are also three in every ten whose aim is to live, but whose movements tend to the land (or place) of death. And for what reason? Because of their excessive endeavours to perpetuate life. But I have heard that he who is skilful in managing the life entrusted to him for a time travels on the land without having to shun rhinoceros or tiger, and enters a host without having to avoid buff coat or sharp weapon. The rhinoceros finds no place in him into which to thrust its horn, nor the tiger a place in which to fix its claws, nor the weapon a place to admit its point. And for what reason? Because there is in him no place of death.

### Chapter 51

道生之，德畜之，物形之，勢成之。
All things are produced by the Tao, and nourished by its outflowing operation.

是以萬物莫不尊道而貴德。
They receive their forms according to the nature of each, and are completed according to the circumstances of their condition. Therefore all things without exception honour the Tao, and exalt its outflowing operation.

道之尊，德之貴，夫莫之命而常自然。 故道生之，德畜之。 長之育之，亭之毒之，蓋之覆之。
This honouring of the Tao and exalting of its operation is not the result of any ordination, but always a spontaneous tribute.

生而不有，為而不恃，
Thus it is that the Tao produces (all things), nourishes them, brings them to their full growth, nurses them, completes them, matures them, maintains them, and overspreads them.

長而不宰。
It produces them and makes no claim to the possession of them;

是謂玄德。
it carries them through their processes and does not vaunt its ability in doing so; it brings them to maturity and exercises no control over them;--this is called its mysterious operation.

### Chapter 52

天下有始，以為天下母。 既得其母，以知其子，既知其子，復守其母，沒身不殆。
(The Tao) which originated all under the sky is to be considered as the mother of them all.

塞其兌，閉其門，
When the mother is found, we know what her children should be.

終身不勤。
When one knows that he is his mother's child, and proceeds to guard (the qualities of) the mother that belong to him, to the end of his life he will be free from all peril.

開其兌，濟其事，終身不救。
Let him keep his mouth closed, and shut up the portals (of his nostrils), and all his life he will be exempt from laborious exertion.

見小曰明，守柔曰強。
Let him keep his mouth open, and (spend his breath) in the promotion of his affairs, and all his life there will be no safety for him.

用其光，
The perception of what is small is (the secret of) clear-sightedness;

復歸其明，
the guarding of what is soft and tender is (the secret of) strength.

無遺身殃，是為習常。
Who uses well his light, Reverting to its (source so) bright, Will from his body ward all blight, And hides the unchanging from men's sight.

### Chapter 53

使我介然有知，行於大道，唯施是畏。 大道甚夷，而民好徑。
If I were suddenly to become known, and (put into a position to) conduct (a government) according to the Great Tao, what I should be most afraid of would be a boastful display.

朝甚除，
The great Tao (or way) is very level and easy;

田甚蕪，倉甚虛；
but people love the by-ways.

服文綵，帶利劍，厭飲食，財貨有餘；
Their court(-yards and buildings) shall be well kept, but their fields shall be ill-cultivated, and their granaries very empty.

是為盜夸。
They shall wear elegant and ornamented robes, carry a sharp sword at their girdle, pamper themselves in eating and drinking, and have a superabundance of property and wealth;--such (princes) may be called robbers and boasters.

非道也哉！
This is contrary to the Tao surely!

### Chapter 54

善建者不拔，
What (Tao's) skilful planter plants Can never be uptorn;

善抱者不脫，
What his skilful arms enfold, From him can ne'er be borne. Sons shall bring in lengthening line, Sacrifices to his shrine.

子孫以祭祀不輟。
Tao when nursed within one's self, His vigour will make true;

修之於身，其德乃真；
And where the family it rules What riches will accrue!

修之於家，其德乃餘；
The neighbourhood where it prevails In thriving will abound; And when 'tis seen throughout the state, Good fortune will be found. Employ it the kingdom o'er, And men thrive all around.

修之於鄉，其德乃長；
In this way the effect will be seen in the person, by the observation of different cases;

修之於國，其德乃豐；
in the family;

修之於天下，其德乃普。
in the neighbourhood;

故以身觀身，以家觀家，以鄉觀鄉，以國觀國，
in the state;

以天下觀天下。
and in the kingdom.

吾何以知天下然哉？
How do I know that this effect is sure to hold thus all under the sky?

以此。
By this (method of observation).

### Chapter 55

含德之厚，比於赤子。
He who has in himself abundantly the attributes (of the Tao) is like an infant.

蜂蠆虺蛇不螫，猛獸不據，攫鳥不搏。
Poisonous insects will not sting him;

骨弱筋柔而握固。
fierce beasts will not seize him;

未知牝牡之合而全作，
birds of prey will not strike him.

精之至也。 終日號而不嗄，
(The infant's) bones are weak and its sinews soft, but yet its grasp is firm.

和之至也。
It knows not yet the union of male and female, and yet its virile member may be excited;--showing the perfection of its physical essence.

知和曰常，
All day long it will cry without its throat becoming hoarse;--showing the harmony (in its constitution).

知常曰明。
To him by whom this harmony is known, (The secret of) the unchanging (Tao) is shown, And in the knowledge wisdom finds its throne.

益生曰祥。
All life-increasing arts to evil turn; Where the mind makes the vital breath to burn, (False) is the strength, (and o'er it we should mourn.)

心使氣曰強。
When things have become strong, they (then) become old, which may be said to be contrary to the Tao.

物壯則老，謂之不道，不道早已。
Whatever is contrary to the Tao soon ends.

### Chapter 56

知者不言， 言者不知。
He who knows (the Tao) does not (care to) speak (about it);

塞其兌，閉其門，挫其銳， 解其分，
he who is (ever ready to) speak about it does not know it.

和其光，
He (who knows it) will keep his mouth shut and close the portals (of his nostrils).

同其塵，
He will blunt his sharp points and unravel the complications of things;

是謂玄同。
he will attemper his brightness, and bring himself into agreement with the obscurity (of others).

故不可得而親，不可得而疏；
This is called 'the Mysterious Agreement.'

不可得而利，不可得而害；
(Such an one) cannot be treated familiarly or distantly;

不可得而貴，不可得而賤。
he is beyond all consideration of profit or injury;

故為天下貴。
of nobility or meanness:--he is the noblest man under heaven.

### Chapter 57

以正治國，以奇用兵，以無事取天下。 吾何以知其然哉？
A state may be ruled by (measures of) correction;

以此。 天下多忌諱，而民彌貧；
weapons of war may be used with crafty dexterity;

民多利器，國家滋昬；
(but) the kingdom is made one's own (only) by freedom from action and purpose.

人多伎巧，奇物滋起；
How do I know that it is so?

法令滋彰，盜賊多有。
By these facts:--In the kingdom the multiplication of prohibitive enactments increases the poverty of the people; the more implements to add to their profit that the people have, the greater disorder is there in the state and clan; the more acts of crafty dexterity that men possess, the more do strange contrivances appear; the more display there is of legislation, the more thieves and robbers there are.

故聖人云：「我無為而民自化，我好靜而民自正，我無事而民自富，我無欲而民自樸。
Therefore a sage has said, 'I will do nothing (of purpose), and the people will be transformed of themselves; 」 I will be fond of keeping still, and the people will of themselves become correct. I will take no trouble about it, and the people will of themselves become rich; I will manifest no ambition, and the people will of themselves attain to the primitive simplicity.'

### Chapter 58

其政悶悶，其民淳淳； 其政察察，其民缺缺。 禍兮福之所倚，福兮禍之所伏。
The government that seems the most unwise, Oft goodness to the people best supplies;

孰知其極？ 其無正。
That which is meddling, touching everything, Will work but ill, and disappointment bring.

正復為奇，
Misery!--happiness is to be found by its side!

善復為妖。
Happiness!--misery lurks beneath it!

人之迷，其日固久。
Who knows what either will come to in the end?

是以聖人方而不割，
Shall we then dispense with correction?

廉而不劌，
The (method of) correction shall by a turn become distortion, and the good in it shall by a turn become evil. The delusion of the people (on this point) has indeed subsisted for a long time.

直而不肆，
Therefore the sage is (like) a square which cuts no one (with its angles);

光而不燿。
(like) a corner which injures no one (with its sharpness). He is straightforward, but allows himself no license; he is bright, but does not dazzle.

### Chapter 59

治人事天，莫若嗇。 夫唯嗇，是謂早服； 早服謂之重積德；
For regulating the human (in our constitution) and rendering the (proper) service to the heavenly, there is nothing like moderation.

重積德則無不克，無不克則莫知其極；
It is only by this moderation that there is effected an early return (to man's normal state).

莫知其極，可以有國；
That early return is what I call the repeated accumulation of the attributes (of the Tao). With that repeated accumulation of those attributes, there comes the subjugation (of every obstacle to such return). Of this subjugation we know not what shall be the limit; and when one knows not what the limit shall be, he may be the ruler of a state.

有國之母，可以長久；
He who possesses the mother of the state may continue long.

是謂深根固柢，長生久視之道。
His case is like that (of the plant) of which we say that its roots are deep and its flower stalks firm:--this is the way to secure that its enduring life shall long be seen.

### Chapter 60

治大國，若烹小鮮。 以道莅天下，其鬼不神；
Governing a great state is like cooking small fish.

非其鬼不神，其神不傷人；
Let the kingdom be governed according to the Tao, and the manes of the departed will not manifest their spiritual energy.

非其神不傷人，聖人亦不傷人。
It is not that those manes have not that spiritual energy, but it will not be employed to hurt men. It is not that it could not hurt men, but neither does the ruling sage hurt them.

夫兩不相傷，故德交歸焉。
When these two do not injuriously affect each other, their good influences converge in the virtue (of the Tao).

### Chapter 61

大國者下流， 天下之交。 天下之牝，
What makes a great state is its being (like) a low-lying, down-flowing (stream);--it becomes the centre to which tend (all the small states) under heaven.

牝常以靜勝牡，以靜為下。
(To illustrate from) the case of all females:--the female always overcomes the male by her stillness.

故大國以下小國，
Stillness may be considered (a sort of) abasement.

則取小國；
Thus it is that a great state, by condescending to small states, gains them for itself;

小國以下大國，則取大國。
and that small states, by abasing themselves to a great state, win it over to them. In the one case the abasement leads to gaining adherents, in the other case to procuring favour.

故或下以取，或下而取。
The great state only wishes to unite men together and nourish them;

大國不過欲兼畜人，小國不過欲入事人。
a small state only wishes to be received by, and to serve, the other.

夫兩者各得其所欲，大者宜為下。
Each gets what it desires, but the great state must learn to abase itself.

### Chapter 62

道者萬物之奧。
Tao has of all things the most honoured place.

善人之寶，
No treasures give good men so rich a grace; Bad men it guards, and doth their ill efface.

不善人之所保。
(Its) admirable words can purchase honour;

美言可以市，尊行可以加人。
(its) admirable deeds can raise their performer above others. Even men who are not good are not abandoned by it.

人之不善，何棄之有？ 故立天子，置三公，
Therefore when the sovereign occupies his place as the Son of Heaven, and he has appointed his three ducal ministers, though (a prince) were to send in a round symbol-of-rank large enough to fill both the hands, and that as the precursor of the team of horses (in the court-yard), such an offering would not be equal to (a lesson of) this Tao, which one might present on his knees.

雖有拱璧以先駟馬，不如坐進此道。 古之所以貴此道者何？
Why was it that the ancients prized this Tao so much?

不曰以求得，有罪以免邪？
Was it not because it could be got by seeking for it, and the guilty could escape (from the stain of their guilt) by it?

故為天下貴。
This is the reason why all under heaven consider it the most valuable thing.

### Chapter 63

為無為，事無事，味無味。
(It is the way of the Tao) to act without (thinking of) acting;

大小多少，報怨以德。
to conduct affairs without (feeling the) trouble of them; to taste without discerning any flavour; to consider what is small as great, and a few as many; and to recompense injury with kindness.

圖難於其易，為大於其細； 天下難事必作於易，天下大事必作於細。
(The master of it) anticipates things that are difficult while they are easy, and does things that would become great while they are small.

是以聖人終不為大，故能成其大。 夫輕諾必寡信，多易必多難。
All difficult things in the world are sure to arise from a previous state in which they were easy, and all great things from one in which they were small.

是以聖人猶難之，
Therefore the sage, while he never does what is great, is able on that account to accomplish the greatest things.

故終無難矣
He who lightly promises is sure to keep but little faith; he who is continually thinking things easy is sure to find them difficult. Therefore the sage sees difficulty even in what seems easy, and so never has any difficulties.

### Chapter 64

其安易持，其未兆易謀。
That which is at rest is easily kept hold of;

其脆易泮，其微易散。
before a thing has given indications of its presence, it is easy to take measures against it;

為之於未有，
that which is brittle is easily broken; that which is very small is easily dispersed. Action should be taken before a thing has made its appearance; order should be secured before disorder has begun.

治之於未亂。 合抱之木，生於毫末；
The tree which fills the arms grew from the tiniest sprout;

九層之臺，起於累土； 千里之行，始於足下。
the tower of nine storeys rose from a (small) heap of earth;

為者敗之，執者失之。
the journey of a thousand li commenced with a single step.

是以聖人無為故無敗，無執故無失。
He who acts (with an ulterior purpose) does harm;

民之從事，常於幾成而敗之。
he who takes hold of a thing (in the same way) loses his hold.

慎終如始，則無敗事。
The sage does not act (so), and therefore does no harm;

是以聖人欲不欲，不貴難得之貨；
he does not lay hold (so), and therefore does not lose his hold. (But) people in their conduct of affairs are constantly ruining them when they are on the eve of success. If they were careful at the end, as (they should be) at the beginning, they would not so ruin them.

學不學，復眾人之所過。
Therefore the sage desires what (other men) do not desire, and does not prize things difficult to get;

以輔萬物之自然，而不敢為。
he learns what (other men) do not learn, and turns back to what the multitude of men have passed by. Thus he helps the natural development of all things, and does not dare to act (with an ulterior purpose of his own).

### Chapter 65

古之善為道者，非以明民，將以愚之。 民之難治，以其智多。
The ancients who showed their skill in practising the Tao did so, not to enlighten the people, but rather to make them simple and ignorant.

故以智治國，國之賊， 不以智治國，國之福。
The difficulty in governing the people arises from their having much knowledge.

知此兩者亦稽式。 常知稽式，是謂玄德。
He who (tries to) govern a state by his wisdom is a scourge to it;

玄德深矣，遠矣，
while he who does not (try to) do so is a blessing.

與物反矣，
He who knows these two things finds in them also his model and rule.

然後乃至大順。
Ability to know this model and rule constitutes what we call the mysterious excellence (of a governor). Deep and far-reaching is such mysterious excellence, showing indeed its possessor as opposite to others, but leading them to a great conformity to him.

### Chapter 66

江海所以能為百谷王者，以其善下之，故能為百谷王。 是以欲上民，必以言下之。 欲先民，必以身後之。
That whereby the rivers and seas are able to receive the homage and tribute of all the valley streams, is their skill in being lower than they;--it is thus that they are the kings of them all.

是以聖人處上而民不重，處前而民不害。 是以天下樂推而不厭，以其不爭，故天下莫能與之爭。
So it is that the sage (ruler), wishing to be above men, puts himself by his words below them, and, wishing to be before them, places his person behind them. In this way though he has his place above them, men do not feel his weight, nor though he has his place before them, do they feel it an injury to them. Therefore all in the world delight to exalt him and do not weary of him. Because he does not strive, no one finds it possible to strive with him.

### Chapter 67

天下皆謂我道大，似不肖。 夫唯大，故似不肖。
All the world says that, while my Tao is great, it yet appears to be inferior (to other systems of teaching).

若肖，久矣其細也夫！ 我有三寶，持而保之。
Now it is just its greatness that makes it seem to be inferior.

一曰慈，二曰儉，三曰不敢為天下先。 慈故能勇，
If it were like any other (system), for long would its smallness have been known!

儉故能廣，
But I have three precious things which I prize and hold fast.

不敢為天下先，故能成器長。
The first is gentleness; the second is economy; and the third is shrinking from taking precedence of others.

今舍慈且勇，
With that gentleness I can be bold;

舍儉且廣，舍後且先，死矣！
with that economy I can be liberal; shrinking from taking precedence of others, I can become a vessel of the highest honour. Now-a-days they give up gentleness and are all for being bold; economy, and are all for being liberal; the hindmost place, and seek only to be foremost;--(of all which the end is) death.

夫慈以戰則勝， 以守則固。
Gentleness is sure to be victorious even in battle, and firmly to maintain its ground.

天將救之，以慈衛之。
Heaven will save its possessor, by his (very) gentleness protecting him.

### Chapter 68

善為士者不武，
He who in (Tao's) wars has skill Assumes no martial port;

善戰者不怒，
He who fights with most good will To rage makes no resort.

善勝敵者不與，
He who vanquishes yet still Keeps from his foes apart; He whose hests men most fulfil Yet humbly plies his art.

善用人者為之下，是謂不爭之德，是謂用人之力， 是謂配天古之極。
Thus we say, 'He ne'er contends, And therein is his might.' Thus we say, 'Men's wills he bends, That they with him unite.' Thus we say, 'Like Heaven's his ends, No sage of old more bright.'

### Chapter 69

用兵有言：「吾不敢為主而為客，不敢進寸而退尺。
A master of the art of war has said, 'I do not dare to be the host (to commence the war);

」是謂行無行，
I prefer to be the guest (to act on the defensive).

攘無臂，扔無敵，
I do not dare to advance an inch;

執無兵。
I prefer to retire a foot.' This is called marshalling the ranks where there are no ranks; baring the arms (to fight) where there are no arms to bare; grasping the weapon where there is no weapon to grasp; advancing against the enemy where there is no enemy.

禍莫大於輕敵，輕敵幾喪吾寶。
There is no calamity greater than lightly engaging in war.

故抗兵相加，哀者勝矣。
To do that is near losing (the gentleness) which is so precious. Thus it is that when opposing weapons are (actually) crossed, he who deplores (the situation) conquers.

### Chapter 70

吾言甚易知，甚易行。 天下莫能知，莫能行。
My words are very easy to know, and very easy to practise;

言有宗，事有君。
but there is no one in the world who is able to know and able to practise them.

夫唯無知，是以不我知。
There is an originating and all-comprehending (principle) in my words, and an authoritative law for the things (which I enforce).

知我者希，則我者貴。
It is because they do not know these, that men do not know me.

是以聖人被褐懷玉。
They who know me are few, and I am on that account (the more) to be prized. It is thus that the sage wears (a poor garb of) hair cloth, while he carries his (signet of) jade in his bosom.

### Chapter 71

知不知上，不知知病。
To know and yet (think) we do not know is the highest (attainment); not to know (and yet think) we do know is a disease.

夫唯病病，是以不病。
It is simply by being pained at (the thought of) having this disease that we are preserved from it.

聖人不病，以其病病，是以不病。
The sage has not the disease. He knows the pain that would be inseparable from it, and therefore he does not have it.

### Chapter 72

民不畏威，則大威至。 無狎其所居，無厭其所生。 夫唯不厭，
When the people do not fear what they ought to fear, that which is their great dread will come on them.

是以不厭。
Let them not thoughtlessly indulge themselves in their ordinary life;

是以聖人自知不自見，
let them not act as if weary of what that life depends on.

自愛不自貴；
It is by avoiding such indulgence that such weariness does not arise.

故去彼取此。
Therefore the sage knows (these things) of himself, but does not parade (his knowledge); loves, but does not (appear to set a) value on, himself. And thus he puts the latter alternative away and makes choice of the former.

### Chapter 73

勇於敢則殺，
He whose boldness appears in his daring (to do wrong, in defiance of the laws) is put to death;

勇於不敢則活。
he whose boldness appears in his not daring (to do so) lives on.

此兩者，或利或害。
Of these two cases the one appears to be advantageous, and the other to be injurious. But

天之所惡，孰知其故？ 是以聖人猶難之。 天之道，不爭而善勝，
When Heaven's anger smites a man, Who the cause shall truly scan?

不言而善應， 不召而自來，
On this account the sage feels a difficulty (as to what to do in the former case).

繟然而善謀。
It is the way of Heaven not to strive, and yet it skilfully overcomes;

天網恢恢，疏而不失。
not to speak, and yet it is skilful in obtaining a reply; does not call, and yet men come to it of themselves. Its demonstrations are quiet, and yet its plans are skilful and effective. The meshes of the net of Heaven are large; far apart, but letting nothing escape.

### Chapter 74

民不畏死，奈何以死懼之？
The people do not fear death;

若使民常畏死，而為奇者，吾得執而殺之，孰敢？
to what purpose is it to (try to) frighten them with death?

常有司殺者殺，夫代司殺者殺，是謂代大匠斲。
If the people were always in awe of death, and I could always seize those who do wrong, and put them to death, who would dare to do wrong?

夫代大匠斲者，希有不傷其手矣。
There is always One who presides over the infliction of death. He who would inflict death in the room of him who so presides over it may be described as hewing wood instead of a great carpenter. Seldom is it that he who undertakes the hewing, instead of the great carpenter, does not cut his own hands!

### Chapter 75

民之饑，以其上食稅之多，是以饑。
The people suffer from famine because of the multitude of taxes consumed by their superiors.

民之難治，以其上之有為，是以難治。
It is through this that they suffer famine.

民之輕死，以其上求生之厚，是以輕死。
The people are difficult to govern because of the (excessive) agency of their superiors (in governing them).

夫唯無以生為者，是賢於貴生。
It is through this that they are difficult to govern. The people make light of dying because of the greatness of their labours in seeking for the means of living. It is this which makes them think light of dying. Thus it is that to leave the subject of living altogether out of view is better than to set a high value on it.

### Chapter 76

人之生也柔弱，其死也堅強。
Man at his birth is supple and weak;

萬物草木之生也柔脆，其死也枯槁。
at his death, firm and strong. (So it is with) all things. Trees and plants, in their early growth, are soft and brittle; at their death, dry and withered.

故堅強者死之徒，柔弱者生之徒。
Thus it is that firmness and strength are the concomitants of death;

是以兵強則不勝，
softness and weakness, the concomitants of life.

木強則兵。
Hence he who (relies on) the strength of his forces does not conquer;

強大處下，
and a tree which is strong will fill the out-stretched arms, (and thereby invites the feller.)

柔弱處上。
Therefore the place of what is firm and strong is below, and that of what is soft and weak is above.

### Chapter 77

天之道，其猶張弓與？ 高者抑之，下者舉之；
May not the Way (or Tao) of Heaven be compared to the (method of) bending a bow?

有餘者損之，不足者補之。 天之道，損有餘而補不足。
The (part of the bow) which was high is brought low, and what was low is raised up.

人之道則不然，
(So Heaven) diminishes where there is superabundance, and supplements where there is deficiency.

損不足以奉有餘。
It is the Way of Heaven to diminish superabundance, and to supplement deficiency. It is not so with the way of man. He takes away from those who have not enough to add to his own superabundance.

孰能有餘以奉天下？
Who can take his own superabundance and therewith serve all under heaven?

唯有道者。
Only he who is in possession of the Tao!

是以聖人為而不恃，功成而不處，其不欲見賢。
Therefore the (ruling) sage acts without claiming the results as his; he achieves his merit and does not rest (arrogantly) in it:--he does not wish to display his superiority.

### Chapter 78

天下莫柔弱於水，而攻堅強者，莫之能勝，其無以易之。
There is nothing in the world more soft and weak than water, and yet for attacking things that are firm and strong there is nothing that can take precedence of it;--for there is nothing (so effectual) for which it can be changed.

弱之勝強，柔之勝剛，天下莫不知莫能行。 是以聖人云：「受國之垢，是謂社稷主； 受國不祥，是為天下王。 」正言若反。
Every one in the world knows that the soft overcomes the hard, and the weak the strong, but no one is able to carry it out in practice. Therefore a sage has said, 'He who accepts his state's reproach, Is hailed therefore its altars' lord; To him who bears men's direful woes They all the name of King accord.' Words that are strictly true seem to be paradoxical.

### Chapter 79

和大怨，必有餘怨， 安可以為善？
When a reconciliation is effected (between two parties) after a great animosity, there is sure to be a grudge remaining (in the mind of the one who was wrong).

是以聖人執左契，
And how can this be beneficial (to the other)?

而不責於人。 有德司契，
Therefore (to guard against this), the sage keeps the left-hand portion of the record of the engagement, and does not insist on the (speedy) fulfilment of it by the other party.

無德司徹。
(So), he who has the attributes (of the Tao) regards (only) the conditions of the engagement, while he who has not those attributes regards only the conditions favourable to himself.

天道無親，常與善人。
In the Way of Heaven, there is no partiality of love; it is always on the side of the good man.

### Chapter 80

小國寡民，
In a little state with a small population, I would so order it, that, though there were individuals with the abilities of ten or a hundred men, there should be no employment of them; I would make the people, while looking on death as a grievous thing, yet not remove elsewhere (to avoid it).

使有什伯之器而不用，
Though they had boats and carriages, they should have no occasion to ride in them; though they had buff coats and sharp weapons, they should have no occasion to don or use them.

使民重死而不遠徙。
I would make the people return to the use of knotted cords (instead of the written characters).

雖有舟輿，無所乘之；
They should think their (coarse) food sweet;

雖有甲兵，無所陳之。
their (plain) clothes beautiful; their (poor) dwellings places of rest; and their common (simple) ways sources of enjoyment.

使人復結繩而用之，甘其食，美其服，安其居，樂其俗。 鄰國相望，雞犬之聲相聞，民至老死，不相往來。
There should be a neighbouring state within sight, and the voices of the fowls and dogs should be heard all the way from it to us, but I would make the people to old age, even to death, not have any intercourse with it.

### Chapter 81

信言不美，
Sincere words are not fine;

美言不信。
fine words are not sincere.

善者不辯，辯者不善。
Those who are skilled (in the Tao) do not dispute (about it);

知者不博，
the disputatious are not skilled in it. Those who know (the Tao) are not extensively learned; the extensively learned do not know it.

博者不知。
The sage does not accumulate (for himself).

聖人不積，
The more that he expends for others, the more does he possess of his own;

既以為人己愈有，
the more that he gives to others, the more does he have himself.

既以與人己愈多。 天之道，利而不害；
With all the sharpness of the Way of Heaven, it injures not;

聖人之道，為而不爭。
with all the doing in the way of the sage he does not strive.
